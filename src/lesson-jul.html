Elijah, Elisha, and the Kings — The Still Small Voice

June 29 – July 26  ·  1 Kings 12–22; 2 Kings; 2 Chronicles

The Stories This Month

A Divided Kingdom: After Solomon's death, Israel split in two — the northern kingdom (Israel) and the southern kingdom (Judah). King after king led the people away from God. Into this wicked world came the prophet Elijah, bold and fearless. He declared a drought as a consequence of Israel's idol worship, and it came. For three and a half years, there was no rain.

Elijah and the Widow: God sent Elijah to a widow in Zarephath who had only enough oil and flour for one last meal for herself and her son. Elijah asked her to feed him first. She obeyed in faith. Her oil and flour never ran out for the entire duration of the drought. Obedience brought miraculous provision.

Mount Carmel: Elijah challenged 450 prophets of the idol Baal to a contest. Both sides prepared a sacrifice, and whichever god answered with fire was the true God. The Baal prophets cried and danced all day — nothing happened. Elijah prayed simply and sincerely, and fire fell from heaven. The people fell on their faces: "The Lord, he is the God."

The Still Small Voice: Afterward, exhausted and afraid, Elijah wanted to die. God didn't come to him in wind or earthquake or fire — He came in "a still small voice," tender and quiet. He told Elijah to rest, eat, and keep going. God meets us in our exhaustion. Elisha carried on after Elijah, performing even more miracles — healing Naaman the leper, multiplying oil for a widow, raising a boy from death.

After the great contest on Mount Carmel, God spoke to Elijah not in fire or thunder — but in a gentle whisper. That quiet voice still speaks today.

Let's Talk About It

  • The widow shared her last meal with Elijah in faith. Has God ever asked you to give something when it felt like you had very little to give?
  • The 450 Baal prophets yelled for hours and nothing happened. Elijah prayed once and fire fell. What does that teach us about how prayer works?
  • Elijah asked God to let him die because he was so tired and afraid. God's response was to give him food, rest, and encouragement. What does that tell you about God's care for us?
  • God spoke to Elijah in "a still small voice" — not in the dramatic wind or fire. How do you hear God's voice in your life?
  • Elisha served Elijah faithfully for years before he received Elijah's spiritual power. What does that teach us about patience and preparation?

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

Elijah is one of the most important figures in all of scripture — not just for his life, but for his role at the end of time. Malachi prophesied that Elijah would return before the great day of the Lord. On the Mount of Transfiguration, Elijah appeared alongside Moses and Jesus, showing he was given as a special witness of the Savior. Elijah's mission of turning hearts — of families to each other, of people to God — points directly to the work Jesus came to do: gathering all of God's children back to their Father. The widow's miracle of unending oil and flour is a picture of Christ's atonement, which never runs out no matter how much we need it.

🤫 Activity: The Still Small Voice

Elijah heard God not in the dramatic wind, earthquake, or fire — but in a gentle whisper. Try this: sit together in complete quiet for one full minute. No talking, no screens, no noise.

Afterward, ask everyone: What did you notice? What thoughts came to you? Is there something you felt you needed to do, say, or change? The Holy Ghost often speaks just like that — a quiet impression, a feeling, a gentle thought. That's the still small voice.
