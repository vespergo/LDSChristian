Isaiah and Jeremiah — Fear Not, I Am With Thee

September 28 – October 25  ·  Isaiah 40–66; Jeremiah 1–20

The Stories This Month

Isaiah 40–55: Comfort My People. These are some of the most beautiful chapters in all scripture. After warning of Israel's coming captivity, God speaks words of tenderness: "Comfort ye, comfort ye my people." He promises He will not forget them: "Can a woman forget her sucking child... yea, they may forget, yet will I not forget thee. Behold, I have graven thee upon the palms of mine hands." In Isaiah 40, God asks: do you think He gets tired? "He giveth power to the faint; and to them that have no might he increaseth strength... They that wait upon the Lord shall renew their strength; they shall mount up with wings as eagles."

Isaiah 53: The Suffering Servant. One of the most specific prophecies of Christ anywhere in scripture. "He was despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief... But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." Written 700 years before the Atonement.

Isaiah 58: True Fasting. God teaches that real religious devotion is not just rituals — it is caring for the poor, feeding the hungry, housing the homeless, being kind. "Is not this the fast that I have chosen? to loose the bands of wickedness, to undo the heavy burdens, and to let the oppressed go free?"

Jeremiah was called as a prophet when he was very young. He said, "I cannot speak: for I am a child." God replied: "Before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee; and before thou camest forth out of the womb I sanctified thee." God knew Jeremiah before he was born — and He knows us too. Jeremiah spent his entire life calling Israel to repent, was thrown in a muddy pit, imprisoned, and ridiculed — and he kept going anyway.

"Is there no balm in Gilead?" (Jeremiah 8:22). God answered through Isaiah and through Jesus Christ: yes, there is healing — and it is enough.

Let's Talk About It

  • Isaiah 40:31 says those who "wait upon the Lord shall renew their strength." What does it mean to "wait upon the Lord"? How is that different from just waiting?
  • God told Israel "I have graven thee upon the palms of mine hands." What do you think that image means? How does it make you feel?
  • Isaiah 53:5 says "with his stripes we are healed." Have you ever felt healed — emotionally, spiritually — because of Jesus Christ? What happened?
  • God told Jeremiah He knew him before he was born. Do you believe God had a plan for your life before you arrived? What might that plan include?
  • Isaiah 58 says real religion means caring for the poor and letting the oppressed go free. What is one thing you can do this week to live that verse?

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

Isaiah 40–55 is called "Deutero-Isaiah" by scholars, but Nephi and Jacob quoted these chapters extensively as direct prophecy of Christ. Isaiah 53 is the centerpiece: the "Suffering Servant" who "hath borne our griefs and carried our sorrows" is Jesus Christ — and every detail matches the Atonement with stunning precision. The image in Isaiah 49:16 — "I have graven thee upon the palms of mine hands" — is especially tender: the Risen Christ showed His hands to Thomas as proof of His resurrection. His wounds are permanent. He still bears the marks of our salvation in His hands. We are literally written there.

✉️ Activity: "Fear Not" Promise Cards

God says "Fear not" or "Be not afraid" over 300 times in scripture. Isaiah is full of them. Together, find and write out three "fear not" promises from Isaiah 40–43 on small cards — one for each person.

Keep your card somewhere you'll see it daily — on the mirror, in your scriptures, by your bed. When something feels scary this month, read your card. Then do what Jeremiah did: keep going anyway.
