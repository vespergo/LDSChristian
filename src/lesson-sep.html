Psalms, Proverbs, and Isaiah — Seeing the Savior

August 31 – September 27  ·  Psalms 102–150; Proverbs; Ecclesiastes; Isaiah 1–35

The Stories This Month

More Psalms: The last half of the Psalms include some of the most beloved words in all of scripture — Psalm 119 is the longest chapter in the Bible, a love letter to God's word. Psalm 139 declares that God knew us before we were born, searches our hearts completely, and is with us wherever we go. These are not just ancient songs. They are the language our hearts reach for when words are hard to find.

Proverbs and Ecclesiastes: Solomon's wisdom books teach practical, everyday godliness. "Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and lean not unto thine own understanding" (Proverbs 3:5). "Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man" (Ecclesiastes 12:13). Wisdom, these books tell us, begins with reverence for God — not with cleverness or experience, but with humility before Him.

Isaiah is often called the Fifth Gospel because he prophesied about Jesus Christ with extraordinary detail — centuries before Christ was born. Isaiah saw the Savior with more clarity than almost any other prophet in the Old Testament. He described His birth, His ministry, His suffering, His death, and His ultimate victory — all in poetry that still moves people to tears.

Isaiah wrote about Christ's birth 700 years before it happened. "For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given" (Isaiah 9:6).

Isaiah's Prophecies About Jesus Christ

  • Isaiah 7:14 "A virgin shall conceive and bear a son" — the birth of Jesus
  • Isaiah 9:6 "Unto us a child is born... Wonderful, Counsellor, the mighty God" — His identity
  • Isaiah 11:1–2 "A rod out of the stem of Jesse" — His lineage through David
  • Isaiah 35:5–6 "The blind shall see... the deaf hear... the lame leap" — His miracles
  • Isaiah 53:3–5 "Despised and rejected... wounded for our transgressions" — His Atonement

Let's Talk About It

  • Psalm 139 says God knew you before you were born and knows every word before you speak it. How does that make you feel — comforted? Nervous? Loved?
  • Proverbs says to "trust in the Lord with all thine heart, and lean not unto thine own understanding." What's the difference between trusting God and trusting your own plans?
  • Isaiah described Christ's birth, death, and resurrection 700 years before they happened. What does that tell you about God's knowledge and plan?
  • Isaiah 9:6 gives Jesus four names: Wonderful Counsellor, the Mighty God, the Everlasting Father, the Prince of Peace. Which of those names means the most to you right now?
  • Ecclesiastes says all of life's busyness is "vanity" — emptiness — except for fearing God and keeping His commandments. Do you agree? Why or why not?

Key Scriptures

Psalm 139:1–2

"O Lord, thou hast searched me, and known me. Thou knowest my downsitting and mine uprising, thou understandest my thought afar off."

Proverbs 3:5–6

"Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and lean not unto thine own understanding. In all thy ways acknowledge him, and he shall direct thy paths."

Isaiah 9:6

"For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace."

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

Isaiah is the most quoted Old Testament prophet in the New Testament. Matthew, Luke, John, Paul — they all turn to Isaiah again and again to explain who Jesus is and what He came to do. Isaiah 53 is so specific that some scholars have called it "the Fifth Gospel": "He is despised and rejected... a man of sorrows... he hath borne our griefs... he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities... with his stripes we are healed." Every line was written 700 years before the cross, yet describes the Atonement with heartbreaking accuracy.

🔍 Activity: Prophecy Detective

Read Isaiah 9:6 aloud. Then read Luke 2:10–11 (the angel announcing Jesus's birth to the shepherds). How many details match?

Now read Isaiah 53:3–5 aloud slowly. As you listen, mark any line that sounds like something from the story of Jesus's suffering. Count how many specific details Isaiah got right — 700 years in advance. Finish by reading together: "With his stripes we are healed." What does that mean for us personally?
