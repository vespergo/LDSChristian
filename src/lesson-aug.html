Ezra, Esther, Job, and the Psalms — Trust God in Every Season

July 27 – August 30  ·  Ezra; Nehemiah; Esther; Job; Psalms 1–86

The Stories This Month

Ezra and Nehemiah: After years of captivity in Babylon, God allowed His people to return to Jerusalem and rebuild. Nehemiah led the rebuilding of the broken city walls in just 52 days — despite enemies, opposition, and threats. When people were discouraged, Nehemiah said: "The God of heaven, he will prosper us." The temple was rebuilt and the scriptures were read aloud to the whole people, who wept as they heard God's word again for the first time in a generation.

Esther was a young Jewish woman living in the Persian empire who became queen — without anyone knowing she was Jewish. When a wicked official named Haman plotted to kill every Jew in the empire, Esther's uncle Mordecai brought her the news and said: "Who knoweth whether thou art come to the kingdom for such a time as this?" Esther risked her life, approached the king uninvited, and saved her people. She discovered that she was exactly where God needed her to be.

Job lost everything — his children, his wealth, his health — in a single terrible season. His friends told him he must have sinned to deserve such suffering. But Job knew he hadn't. He struggled, questioned, cried out — and he never stopped speaking directly to God. Through it all he said: "I know that my redeemer liveth." In the end, God appeared and restored everything. Job didn't get explanations, but he got something better: an encounter with God Himself.

The Psalms are the songbook of Israel — 150 songs covering every human emotion. Joy, grief, anger, trust, praise, doubt, worship. The Psalms teach us that it is okay to bring everything to God — exactly as we are.

"I know that my redeemer liveth, and that he shall stand at the latter day upon the earth." Job trusted God even when he had no answers — only questions.

Let's Talk About It

  • Nehemiah rebuilt broken walls in 52 days, despite enemies trying to stop him. What "broken thing" in your life might God want to help you rebuild?
  • Esther said "if I perish, I perish" before risking her life to save her people. Have you ever done something brave for someone else, even when it was scary?
  • Job's friends said his suffering was his own fault. But they were wrong. Why do you think people sometimes blame others for their hard times?
  • Job said "I know that my Redeemer liveth" in the middle of his worst suffering. How do you hold onto faith when things are very hard?
  • The Psalms express anger, sadness, joy, and praise — every emotion. Which emotion do you need to bring to God today?

Key Scriptures

Esther 4:14

"Who knoweth whether thou art come to the kingdom for such a time as this?"

Job 19:25

"For I know that my redeemer liveth, and that he shall stand at the latter day upon the earth."

Psalm 23:1, 4

"The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want... Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil: for thou art with me."

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

Job's declaration — "I know that my Redeemer liveth" — is one of the clearest Old Testament testimonies of Jesus Christ. Job saw that his ultimate rescue would not come from answers, but from a living Redeemer who would stand on the earth. Esther's willingness to risk everything to save her people is a picture of Christ's mission: He stepped forward, knowing the cost, to save us. The Psalms include some of the most specific prophecies of Christ's suffering: Psalm 22 describes crucifixion in detail a thousand years before it happened — "They pierce my hands and my feet... they cast lots for my clothing." Every line of anguish in Psalm 22 points to Calvary.

📝 Activity: Write Your Own Psalm

The Psalms were prayers and songs written honestly to God — including feelings of fear, sadness, anger, gratitude, and praise. David didn't pretend to be okay when he wasn't. He brought his real feelings to God.

Each person writes their own short "psalm" — at least 4 lines. Start with how you're really feeling right now. Be honest. End with a statement of trust in God, even if it's a small one. Share them if you want, or keep them private.
