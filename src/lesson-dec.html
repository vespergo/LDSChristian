Micah, Zechariah, Malachi, and Christmas — All Roads Lead to Christ

November 30 – December 27  ·  Micah; Haggai; Zechariah; Malachi + Christmas

The Stories This Month

Micah asked the famous question: "But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting." When the wise men came to King Herod asking where the Messiah would be born, the priests quoted this exact verse. A prophet 700 years earlier had written the street address of the Savior.

Haggai and Zechariah called Israel to rebuild the temple after returning from Babylon. Zechariah's visions included a detailed picture of Christ's triumphal entry into Jerusalem ("thy King cometh unto thee, meek, and riding upon an ass"), the betrayal for thirty pieces of silver, the piercing of His hands, and the Savior standing on the Mount of Olives at His return. These were not vague poetic images. They were exact.

Malachi was the last prophet before 400 years of silence — until John the Baptist. He promised that a messenger would prepare the way for the Lord. He promised Elijah would return before the great day. He promised tithes paid in faith would open the windows of heaven. And his final words turned the hearts of children to their fathers and fathers to their children — the work of sealing families together through ordinances.

Christmas: And then — silence. For four centuries, no prophet spoke. Then an angel appeared to a young woman named Mary. "Fear not," he said. "For thou hast found favour with God. And, behold, thou shalt conceive in thy womb, and bring forth a son, and shalt call his name Jesus." Every promise, every prophecy, every sacrifice, every story in this entire year of study — all of it was pointing to this moment.

A whole year of Old Testament stories — all of them pointing forward to one night in Bethlehem, when every promise God ever made came to life.

Let's Talk About It

  • Micah named the exact town where Jesus would be born 700 years in advance. How does that level of precision affect your faith in God's plan?
  • Malachi ended the Old Testament with a promise that Elijah would return and turn hearts of parents and children to each other. Why do you think God placed that promise at the very end?
  • There were 400 years of silence between Malachi and the New Testament. How do you think faithful people stayed strong during that silence?
  • Every story this year — from creation to Christmas — was pointing to Jesus. Which Old Testament story connected most to Christ for you this year?
  • The angel told Mary "Fear not." What fears do you bring to this Christmas season? How does the arrival of Jesus help with them?

✝ This Is How the Old Testament Ends — and Begins Again

The Old Testament is not a collection of unrelated stories. It is one continuous story — God preparing the world, over thousands of years, for the arrival of His Son. Every prophet spoke of Him. Every sacrifice pointed to Him. Every covenant was made through Him. Every deliverance was a preview of His. When the angel said to Mary "Fear not" and announced the birth of Jesus, it was the moment every promise in this entire year came true. The child in the manger was the burning bush, the Red Sea, the still small voice, the suffering servant, the dry bones made alive — He was all of it. He was always the point. He still is.

⭐ Activity: Year-End Prophecy Match

As you celebrate Christmas, read the Nativity story from Luke 2:1–20. After each scene, pause and ask: "Which Old Testament prophet said this would happen?"

  • Born in Bethlehem → Micah 5:2
  • Born of a virgin → Isaiah 7:14
  • Angels announce His birth → Isaiah 9:6
  • A star appears → Numbers 24:17

Then share one thing you learned about Jesus this year from the Old Testament that you didn't know before. Close by singing "Joy to the World" — which is based on Psalm 98 — and remembering: the whole year was always leading here.
