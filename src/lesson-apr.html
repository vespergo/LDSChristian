Exodus and Easter — God Delivers His People

March 30 – April 26  ·  Exodus 7–20; 24; 31–34

The Stories This Month

Easter: April begins with the most important event in history — the Resurrection of Jesus Christ. Before we read about Moses and Pharaoh, we remember that God's greatest act of deliverance was not at the Red Sea. It was at an empty tomb.

The Ten Plagues: Moses returned to Egypt and stood before the most powerful man in the world. "Let my people go," he said. Pharaoh refused — ten times. Ten plagues swept Egypt: water turned to blood, frogs, lice, flies, livestock disease, boils, hail, locusts, darkness, and finally the death of every firstborn in homes without the blood of a lamb on their doorposts. The Israelites put that blood on their doors. Death "passed over" them. They were free.

Crossing the Red Sea: With Pharaoh's army thundering behind them and the sea ahead, Israel panicked. Moses raised his staff. God parted the waters and Israel walked through on dry ground. When the Egyptian army followed, the waters came crashing back. Israel stood on the other shore, safe, singing the first recorded song in scripture.

The Ten Commandments: At Mount Sinai, God descended in fire and thunder and gave His people ten foundational laws. These are not restrictions designed to make life hard — they are a description of what life in God's family looks like. Love God. Honor your parents. Don't steal, lie, or covet. They still matter today.

Israel's crossing of the Red Sea is one of history's most dramatic moments — and one of scripture's greatest pictures of salvation.

The Ten Commandments at a Glance

1. No other gods before God
2. No idols or false images
3. Don't misuse God's name
4. Keep the Sabbath holy
5. Honor your father and mother
6. Do not murder
7. Do not commit adultery
8. Do not steal
9. Do not lie
10. Do not covet

Let's Talk About It

  • Easter comes first in April's study. Why do you think the Old Testament stories of deliverance matter even more when you know how the story ends — with the Resurrection?
  • Israel was terrified at the Red Sea — armies behind, water ahead. When have you felt "trapped" between two hard things? What happened?
  • The Passover lamb's blood protected the firstborn. How does that image help you understand what Jesus Christ did for us?
  • Which of the Ten Commandments do you think is hardest for people today, and why?
  • After crossing the Red Sea, the Israelites sang a song to God. When did you last thank God for saving or helping you?

Key Scriptures

Exodus 12:13

"And the blood shall be to you for a token upon the houses where ye are: and when I see the blood, I will pass over you, and the plague shall not be upon you to destroy you."

Exodus 14:13–14

"Fear ye not, stand still, and see the salvation of the Lord... The Lord shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace."

Exodus 20:2–3

"I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage. Thou shalt have no other gods before me."

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

The Passover lamb is one of the clearest symbols of Christ in the entire Old Testament. Just as the lamb's blood protected Israel from death, Christ's blood protects us from spiritual death. Paul wrote: "For even Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" (1 Corinthians 5:7). The Red Sea crossing is also a symbol of baptism — Israel passed through water and emerged on the other side as a covenant people, just as we pass through baptism and emerge as covenant members of God's family. And Easter — the Resurrection — is the ultimate deliverance: death itself could not hold the Son of God.

📜 Activity: Stone Tablet Commandments

Cut out two "stone tablet" shapes from paper or cardboard. Write the Ten Commandments on them — five on each tablet. Decorate them to look like ancient stone.

Then talk about this: Jesus summed up all ten commandments in two sentences — "Love God" and "Love your neighbor." How do all ten commandments fit into those two? Try matching each commandment to one of Jesus's two big rules.
