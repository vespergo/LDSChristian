Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, and the Minor Prophets — Stand Firm

October 26 – November 29  ·  Jeremiah 31–38; Lamentations; Ezekiel; Daniel; Hosea; Joel; Amos; Obadiah; Jonah

The Stories This Month

Jeremiah's New Covenant: Even as Jerusalem was falling, Jeremiah wrote about a day when God would make a new covenant — not written on stone tablets but on hearts. "I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts; and will be their God, and they shall be my people." This prophecy pointed directly to the gospel of Jesus Christ.

Ezekiel's Valley of Dry Bones: Ezekiel saw a vision of a valley filled with dry, bleached bones — a picture of Israel in captivity: dead, scattered, hopeless. God asked: "Can these bones live?" Then God commanded Ezekiel to speak to the bones. As he did, they came together, flesh covered them, and breath entered them. They stood up — a vast army. God explained: this is what I will do for my people. No situation is too far gone. God can bring life to what seems completely dead.

Daniel, Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego were young Israelites taken captive to Babylon. They refused to eat the king's food that violated their religious law. They refused to bow to a golden statue — even when threatened with a furnace. Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego were thrown in. And a fourth person appeared walking with them in the flames: one "like the Son of God." They came out without even the smell of smoke. Daniel later survived a night in a den of lions.

Jonah ran from God, was swallowed by a great fish, prayed from inside it, and was spit out on shore. He obeyed, went to Nineveh, preached — and the whole city repented. Then Jonah was angry that God forgave them. God's lesson: His love is bigger than we think, and He cares about everyone.

Daniel spent a night in a den of lions for praying to God. By morning, he was unharmed — his faith protected him where no human power could.

Let's Talk About It

  • Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego told the king: "Our God is able to deliver us — but even if He doesn't, we still won't bow." What does that kind of faith look like? Have you ever had to take a stand like that?
  • Ezekiel's dry bones came to life when he spoke God's word. Is there an area of your life that feels "dry" or "dead" — where you need God to breathe new life?
  • Jonah ran from God, but God kept pursuing him. Has God ever "followed" you when you were trying to get away from Him? What happened?
  • Jonah was upset that God forgave Nineveh — his enemy. Is there anyone you find it hard to want God to bless or forgive?
  • Daniel prayed three times a day even when it was illegal. How regular is your prayer life? What would help you make prayer more consistent?

Key Scriptures

Jeremiah 31:33

"But this shall be the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel; After those days, saith the Lord, I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts; and will be their God, and they shall be my people."

Ezekiel 37:3–4

"And he said unto me, Son of man, can these bones live? And I answered, O Lord God, thou knowest. Again he said unto me, Prophesy upon these bones, and say unto them, O ye dry bones, hear the word of the Lord."

Daniel 3:17–18

"Our God whom we serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace... But if not, be it known unto thee, O king, that we will not serve thy gods, nor worship the golden image."

✝ How This Points to Jesus Christ

The fourth person walking in the fiery furnace — "like the Son of God" — was almost certainly the pre-mortal Jesus Christ, standing with His faithful servants in their moment of greatest danger. Ezekiel's dry bones vision is a prophecy of resurrection: the same power that raised those dead bones to life will one day raise every person who has ever lived. Jeremiah's "new covenant" written on hearts was fulfilled in the New Testament through the Holy Ghost, given to every baptized member of Christ's church. And Jonah's three days inside the fish was the sign Jesus pointed to as the sign of His own death and resurrection: "As Jonah was three days and three nights in the whale's belly; so shall the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth" (Matthew 12:40).

🦴 Activity: Dry Bones Comeback

On a piece of paper, draw a simple skeleton — or just a pile of scattered bones. Label it with something in your life or in the world that seems hopeless, dead, or dried up.

Then read Ezekiel 37:3–4 aloud. God asked Ezekiel: "Can these bones live?" He didn't tell him yes — He told him to speak faith to them. On your drawing, write the words: "O dry bones, hear the word of the Lord." Then write one thing you believe God could do with this situation. Close with prayer.
